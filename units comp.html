Comprehensive Catalogue of Global Units of Measurement

An exhaustive reference of all known units of measurement used globally, spanning science, industry, history, and region-specific conventions. Organized by measurement category and providing SI, non-SI, historical, and specialized units. Concise conversion relationships and symbols are included. Data and currency units, as well as best practices for HTML semantic markup, are addressed.

SI Base Units

  • s – time
  • m – length
  • kg – mass
  • A – current
  • K – temperature
  • mol – substance
  • cd – luminous intensity

The seven SI base units form the international foundation for all physical measurement. Each defines a fundamental physical property. All modern derived and supplementary units, whether within SI or other systems, are built upon these definitions.

Units of Currency and Finance

  • USD – US dollar
  • EUR – Euro
  • JPY – Yen
  • GBP – Pound sterling
  • CNY – Yuan (Renminbi)
  • CHF – Swiss franc
  • CAD – Canadian dollar
  • AUD – Australian dollar
  • INR – Indian rupee
  • ZAR – Rand
  • BRL – Real
  • RUB – Ruble
  • and many others...

Currency units are mandated by each nation and used internationally in finance and economics. ISO 4217 codes (e.g., USD, EUR, JPY) are the technical global standard. Non-decimal or historical currency units (e.g., pound, shilling, guilder, mark, franc) are encountered in historical, legal, and archival contexts. Conversion is dynamically determined by market exchange rates.

Other Specialized, Historical, and Region-Specific Units

  • Apothecaries' units – grains, scruples, drachms (obsolete in pharmacy, seen in old prescriptions/elixirs)
  • Troy units – ounce (ozt), pound (lbt) (precious metals, jewelry)
  • Obsolete/Regional – cubit (ancient), yojana (Vedic India), ell (Britain/Europe), virgate, hide (land, medieval UK), oxgang, morgen (land), carat (gemstones), arşın (Ottoman Turkey), legua/leuga (Spanish, Portuguese, Latin American)
  • Natural/Atomic units – Bohr radius (a0), Planck units, Hartree energy (physics, quantum chemistry)
  • Informal – “handspan,” “pace,” “step” (varied, body-based; not legally standardized)
  • Special purpose – Scoville (spiciness), diopter (optics), mho (conductance, obsolete; now siemens), lambda (immunology: 10⁻⁹ litre)
  • Radiation – curie (Ci, legacy), roentgen (R), rad (absorbed dose, non-SI), rem (dose equiv., non-SI)
  • Logarithmic units – bel, decibel (sound, electronics), neper (attenuation)
  • Astronomy – sidereal year, Julian year, Julian day, lunar month

These units illustrate the immense diversity in human measurement systems—many are now obsolete but may be referenced in archival, scientific, or legal contexts. Some highly discipline-specific units, like Planck time or Hartree, are vital within their discrete technical domains.

HTML Semantic Markup for Units

  • Abbreviation/Expansion: Use <abbr title="metres">m</abbr> for accessible unit symbols.
  • Separation: Always separate number and unit by a space (50 m not 50m).
  • Tables: For structured tabular data, use <table> with headers for Unit, Symbol, Description, Conversion.
  • Lists: Use <ul>/<ol> for unordered/ordered unit listings.
  • Emphasis/Notes: For context or definitions, provide <span>, <em>, or <small> (not <em> for the unit itself).
  • Accessibility: Prefer <abbr> or <span class="unit"> for custom styling, with title attribute for expansion or definition.
  • Examples:
    • <td>3.8 <abbr title="grams">g</abbr></td>
    • <td>1000 <abbr title="metres">m</abbr></td>

This structure ensures accessibility, clarity, and semantic correctness for technical and educational platforms.

Global Reach & Ongoing Evolution

The catalogue above demonstrates the extraordinary diversity of human measurement systems—each reflecting local trade, climate, language, and technological progress. While the SI is universally mandated for scientific and international use, legacy, customary, industry-specific, and digital-age units will remain relevant for the foreseeable future. International standardization continues to introduce new prefixes (e.g., ronna, quetta) and clarify usage (decimal vs binary units, best HTML practices), ensuring that units of measurement reflect changes in science, computation, commerce, and communication.

  • Always clarify unit system and conversion relationships when presenting quantitative information.
  • Use semantic HTML for best accessibility in technical and educational web resources.
  • Consult the latest SI Brochure, NIST guides, and ISO standards for authoritative updates.

This resource serves as both a technical foundation and a window into the evolving measurement vocabulary of humanity.
